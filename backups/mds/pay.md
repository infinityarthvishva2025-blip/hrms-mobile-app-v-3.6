Wood and Douglas (2019) proffered that the illusion of the conspiracy theory must be sinister in nature and cast an immense amount of doubt on what the majority of the public believes to be true. Fenster (1999) believed that the essence of a belief in a conspiracy theory was the desire to alert and persuade the masses that there is truth out there that needs to  be  uncovered.  It  was  the  focus  of  the  present  study  to determine  the  relationships among  personality  characteris-tics,  paranormal  beliefs,  and political  ideologies  of  people who endorse conspiracy theories.Interestingly, conspiracy theories are not a new phenom-enon.  A  massive  fire  in  Rome  in  64  CE  was  blamed  on Emperor Nero, and Nero tried to extinguish these rumors by starting  a  conspiracy  of  his  own  by  blaming  Christians (Pagán, 2020). Conspiracy theories were also popular in the Americas in the 17th century, as the Puritans believed that they were being attacked by disciples of the devil. Further, it was their belief that when Christianity began to spread in Europe,  the  devil had  moved into  the  New  World  (Butter, 2020).   Butter   observed   that   the   history   of   conspiracy theories   has   progressed   from   orthodox   to   heterodox knowledge. By orthodox, Butter meant that the conspiracy was  originally  seen  aslegitimate,  and  by  heterodox,  he meant that conspiracy theories had become stigmatized and were  no  longer  a  majority  belief.  Butter  also  noted  that conspiracy theories have nevertheless been influential over the past two centuries, and they have shaped perceptions of many  critical  events  in  history.  Butter  used  the  Kennedy assassination  and  the  moon  landing  as  well-known  exam-ples of the influence conspiracy theories have had in recent history.  Butter  emphasized  that  conspiracy  theories  con-tinue  to  be  heterodox  knowledge,  particularly  because  un-censored social media often makes it difficult for people to differentiate what is true and what is not in public discourse. Van  Prooijen  and  van  Vugt  (2018)  hypothesized  that conspiracy  theories  could  be  evolutionary  by-products  of more basic psychological mechanisms. They proposed that a by-product does not have adaptive features itself, but it can be ‘carried along’ with certain adaptive mechanisms that do possess  adaptive  features,  like  the  association  between creativity  and  schizophrenia. A brief review of the characteristics of conspiracy theory believersAt  least  three  characteristic  categories  have  emerged  in association   with   beliefs    in   conspiracy   theories:   (1) Personality disorders, and trait domains and facets (from the Diagnostic  and  Statistical  Manual  of  Mental  Disorders[DSM-5-TR; American Psychiatric Association, 2022]); (2) Paranormal beliefs; and (3) Political ideology. Personality disorders, trait domains, and facets Swami, Weis, Layet al. (2016) investigated the relationship between the five personality disorder trait domains and their 25 facets with conspiracy beliefs in 259 adult participants. The  domains and facets were  measured by the  Personality Inventory for DSM-5(PID-5; Krueger,  Derringer,  Markonet  al.,  2012).  They  found  significant  and  positive  correla-tions   between   every   domain   and   conspiracy   beliefs: Psychoticism (r= .49), Disinhibition (r= .42), Antagonism (r = .41), Negative Affectivity (r= .40), and Detachment (r= .34). They also found significant and positive correlations between all of the 25 facets and conspiracy beliefs (median r= .38; range Risk Taking [r= .24] to Unusual Beliefs and Experiences [r= .48]). It is interesting to note that the latter facet  had  the  strongest  correlation  with  conspiracy  beliefs (yet less than the criterion for a strong correlation [r > .50]). This  finding  may  be  deemed  specious  at  best  because,  by definition,  conspiracy  beliefs  are  almost  always  unusual beliefs. The latter phenomenon is inherently redundant in its nature and reveals little about the essential characteristics of those who believe in conspiracy theories. Further, Swami et al. (2016) found  no  significant  differences  in  conspiracy beliefs  as a function of the participants’ sex, ethnicity, or education.Bowes,  Costello,  Ma, andLilienfeld(2020)  examined four  adult  samples  in  a  total  of  1,927,  investigating  the relationship between the  PID-5, other measures of person-ality, and three measures of conspiracy beliefs. They found significant  and  positive  correlations  between  the  PID-5 domains and facets, other personality traits, and conspiracy beliefs.  However,  none  of  the  correlations  had  a  strong effect  size  (r< .50). The authors concluded, “... the rela-tively  modest  or  weak  effect  sizes  we  have  reported,  the picture  we  offer  here  is  best  regarded  as  a  fuzzy  sketch, ideally one to be fleshed out in future research” (p. 13).Teličák and Halama (2021) also investigated the rela-tionship  between  the  PID-5  domains,  spirituality,  religi-osity, and conspiracy beliefs in 829 adults. Consistent with Bowes et al.’s (2020) findings, there were only weak rela-tionships  among  the  variables,  including  spirituality  and religiosity. They also concluded that conspiracy beliefs “... are a complex multifactor phenomenon and a consequence of the interaction between personality, cognitive, social, and other factors” (p. 185).Furnham  and  Grover  (2022)  explored  the  relationship between   a   measure   of   14 DSMpersonality   disorders (SCATI; Coolidge,  Segal,  Cahill,  &  Simenson,  2010)  and conspiracy beliefs in 475 British adults. They found that the Schizotypal,   Paranoid,   and   Borderline   PD   scales   best predicted conspiracy beliefs; however, only the Schizotypal scale  reached  a  medium  effect  size.  They  concluded  that conspiracy  beliefs  are  associated  with  a  wide  range  of personality disorders, albeit weakly or at best modestly. In  summary,  the  relationship  between  the DSM-5-TRpersonality disorders, trait domains, and facets, and beliefs in  conspiracy  theories  has  been  found  to  be  complex, multifactorial, and unrevealing at best. As noted previously, studies   have   found   the   facet   of   Unusual   Beliefs   and Experiences, which is part of the  Psychoticism domain, to be the most highly correlated with conspiracy beliefs, which is simply supplying a redundant synonym.  Paranormal beliefsA  paranormal  belief  can  be  described  as  a  scientifically impossible phenomenon, such as precognition, psychokine-sis, witchcraft, and aliens, etc. (Darwin, Neave, & Holmes, 2011). According to Darwin et al., belief in the paranormal has also been described as a multidimensional construct that merges  the  ideas  of  magical  thinking,  superstition,  and religious beliefs. Lobato, Mendoza,  Sims, andChin(2014) examined paranormal and conspiracy beliefs in 455 college students. Their paranormal belief scale consisted of 14 items measuring various beliefs in ESP (e.g., telekinesis, fortune telling,  séances,  etc.),  geomancy  (e.g.,  Bermuda  Triangle, crystal  power,  etc.),  astromancy  (e.g.,  astrology,  solar  and lunar  influences,  etc.),  and  fantastic  beasts  (e.g.,  Bigfoot, aliens,  chupacabra,  etc.).  They  found  a  strong  positive correlation   between   paranormal   beliefs   and   conspiracy beliefs  (r=  .52)  and  concluded  that  these  related  beliefs were manifestations  of  “similar  intuitive  cognitions”  (p. 624).  Further,  they noted  that  although  there  were  content differences between these beliefs at a superficial level, they may have an underlying cognitive foundation. Swami et al. (2016) later argued that paranormal beliefs were “akin” to conspiracy beliefs, and this set of anomalous beliefs allowed people to more easily believe in conspiracy theories. Political ideologyVan Prooijen, Krouwel, and Pollet(2015), in a  study with 268 adult participants, found that political extremism (e.g., extreme conservatism and extreme liberalism) and conspir-acy  beliefs  were  significantly  correlated.  Confirming  their original hypothesis, those who held extreme political beliefs were more likely to believein conspiracy theories than those with more moderate political beliefs. The authors speculated that extreme ways of thinking, seeing things as ‘black-and-white,’ contributed to the susceptibility to conspiracy the-ories.
